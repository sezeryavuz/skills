# Startup Analysis Criteria

The nine dimensions this skill analyses, what evidence each one demands, and how to push a
vague answer toward a specific one. Used in Phases 1–3.

## Where these come from

Dimensions 1–6 are the founder-side reading of the six forcing questions `office-hours` asks in
its startup mode. Dimensions 7–9 — premise integrity, distribution reality, narrative integrity —
are what this skill adds, because they are what a founder must manage *between* diagnostic
sessions.

Knowing which is which matters operationally: on 1–6 you are helping the founder prepare for a
conversation that will happen anyway, so a weak answer is a rehearsal problem. On 7–9 no
external pass is coming, so a weak answer stays weak until this skill catches it.

## Operating principles

These shape every judgment below.

**Interest is not demand.** Waitlists, signups, "that's interesting" — none of it counts.
Behavior counts. Money counts. Panic when it breaks counts.

**The status quo is the real competitor.** Not another startup — the spreadsheet-and-messages
workaround the user already lives with. If the current solution is genuinely *nothing*, that
usually means the problem is not painful enough to act on.

**The user's words beat the founder's pitch.** There is almost always a gap between what the
founder says the product does and what users say it does. The users' version is the truth; if
the best customers describe the value differently than the copy does, the copy is wrong.

**Watch, don't demo.** Guided walkthroughs teach nothing about real usage. Sitting behind
someone while they struggle — and biting your tongue — teaches everything.

**Narrow beats wide, early.** The smallest version someone pays for this week is worth more
than the full platform vision.

**Specificity is the only currency.** "Enterprises in healthcare" is not a customer. "Everyone
needs this" means the founder cannot find anyone.

## 1. Demand reality

*The strongest available evidence that someone genuinely wants this.*

Rank what you are given — these are not equivalent:

founder belief → casual interest → waitlist behavior → repeated usage → workflow dependency →
payment → expansion → urgency → **a user who becomes upset when the product is unavailable**

Push until you hear specific behavior: someone paying, someone expanding usage, someone who
would have to scramble if the product vanished.

Red flags: "people say it's interesting", "we got 500 waitlist signups", "investors are excited
about the space". None of these are demand, and a founder who leads with them usually knows it.

## 2. Status quo

*What the target user does today without the product.*

Capture the current workflow, existing tools, manual work, spreadsheets, message threads,
consultants, internal processes — and the cost: hours, dollars, risk, frustration,
organizational inertia.

Red flag: "nothing — there's no solution, that's why the opportunity is so big." If nobody is
doing anything about the problem, the problem is usually not urgent.

## 3. Desperate specificity

*Move from an audience category to an identifiable human.*

Capture a name or concrete persona, role, company type, actual context, the triggering event,
the consequence of failure, what gets this person promoted or blocked or embarrassed or fired,
and the exact moment the problem becomes urgent.

"SMBs", "developers", "healthcare companies" are filters, not people — you cannot email a
category.

The consequence should match the domain: B2B tools name career impact; consumer tools name
daily pain or a social moment; hobby and open-source tools name the weekend project that gets
unblocked.

## 4. Narrowest wedge

*The smallest outcome someone would pay for or depend on now.*

Separate the long-term platform vision · the initial wedge · the first workflow · the first
user · the first distribution channel · the first paid or behaviorally validated outcome.

Push until the founder describes something shippable in days that someone would pay for — one
feature, one workflow, sometimes just a weekly email or a single automation.

Red flags: "we need the full platform before anyone can really use it", "we could strip it down
but then it wouldn't be differentiated." Both usually mean the founder is attached to the
architecture rather than the value. If no one can get value from a smaller version, that is
evidence the value proposition is not yet clear — not evidence that the product must be bigger.

Useful bonus push: what would this look like if the user had to do *nothing* — no login, no
integration, no setup?

## 5. Observation and surprise

*Whether the founder has watched unassisted user behavior.*

Capture what the user actually did, what surprised the founder, where behavior contradicted
expectations, unintended uses, friction hidden by demos, and evidence that came from watching
rather than asking.

Red flags: "we sent out a survey", "we did some demo calls", "nothing surprising, it's going as
expected." Surveys lie, demos are theater, and "as expected" means the founder is filtering
through existing assumptions.

The gold is users doing something the product was not designed for — often the real product
trying to emerge.

If no observation exists at all, that is an **observation gap**: label it and create a concrete
observation assignment. It is never repairable by rewriting.

## 6. Future-fit

*Whether changes over the next three years make the product more or less essential.*

Require a specific causal thesis about how the user's world changes and why that change makes
this particular product more necessary.

Reject rising-tide arguments every competitor could make equally: "AI will keep improving",
"the market is growing 20% a year", "digital transformation is accelerating." A growth rate is
not a vision.

## 7. Premise integrity

*The startup decomposed into explicit, falsifiable beliefs.*

For each premise record: the claim · current evidence · contradicting evidence · confidence ·
**what would prove it wrong** · its kind (product, market, distribution, technical, or founder
belief) · and its state (agreed, revised, or unresolved).

The falsification field is the one founders skip and the one that does the work. A premise with
no disconfirming test is not a premise — it is an identity, and it will survive contact with
any amount of evidence.

## 8. Distribution reality

*How the product reaches users.*

Capture the acquisition channel, sales motion, integration path, delivery mechanism, initial
access point, **who authorizes purchase**, **who experiences the pain**, who owns
implementation, time to first value, and distribution dependencies.

"We will market it" is not a distribution plan. Where the payer and the sufferer are different
people, say so explicitly — that gap is a distribution premise, and it sinks otherwise good
products.

## 9. Narrative integrity

*Whether the founder-facing representation is honest and clear.*

Test whether it is specific, evidence-aware, internally consistent, clear about what is known
versus hypothesized, free of unsupported superlatives, centered on the user rather than the
architecture, honest about stage and uncertainty — and **consistent across every artifact**:
memo, pitch, landing page, application, demo script, README, design document.

This measures clarity and integrity, not investment worthiness. A pre-product startup can score
4 here by being precise about how little it knows.

## Stage routing

Not every dimension needs equal weight in every session. The forcing-question routing used by
`office-hours` is a good default for where to spend the founder's attention:

| Stage | Focus dimensions |
|---|---|
| Pre-product | 1 Demand, 2 Status quo, 3 Specificity |
| Has users, no revenue | 2 Status quo, 4 Wedge, 5 Observation |
| Has paying customers | 4 Wedge, 5 Observation, 6 Future-fit |
| Pure engineering / infra | 2 Status quo, 4 Wedge |

Dimensions 7–9 apply at every stage — a pre-product startup has premises, a distribution
question, and a narrative just as much as a revenue-stage one.

**Intrapreneurial adaptation:** reframe wedge as "the smallest demo that gets your sponsor to
greenlight this", and future-fit as "does this survive a reorg, or die when your champion
leaves?"

If earlier answers already cover a later dimension, skip it. Only ask what is not yet clear.

## How to push

The first answer to any of these is usually the polished version; the real one arrives after
the second or third push. But push as a coach, not an examiner — the goal is the founder's own
clarity.

| Founder says | Weak response | Better response |
|---|---|---|
| "An AI tool for developers" | "That's a big market — what kind of tool?" | "What specific task does a specific developer waste two hours a week on that this eliminates? Name the person." |
| "Everyone I've talked to loves the idea" | "Encouraging — who have you talked to?" | "Loving an idea is free. Has anyone offered to pay? Asked when it ships? Been angry when the prototype broke?" |
| "We need the full platform first" | "What would a stripped-down version look like?" | "If nobody can get value from something smaller, that usually means the value isn't clear yet. What would someone pay for this week?" |
| "The market is growing 20% a year" | "Strong tailwind — how will you capture it?" | "Every competitor cites the same stat. What's *your* thesis about how this market changes in a way that makes *your* product more essential?" |
| "We want onboarding to be more seamless" | "What does onboarding look like now?" | "'Seamless' is a feeling, not a feature. Which step causes drop-off? What's the rate? Have you watched someone go through it?" |

Two habits keep this constructive rather than combative: **take a position and say what
evidence would change it**, and **challenge the strongest version of the founder's claim**, not
a convenient weak one.

Avoid hedges that carry no information — "that's an interesting approach", "there are many ways
to think about this", "that could work", "you might want to consider." They feel supportive and
teach nothing. Naming a real strength precisely is more useful than any of them: "that's the
most specific demand evidence here — a customer calling when it broke."

## Truth classification

Every important claim gets exactly one label, recorded with its source:

observed fact · quantitative evidence · customer quote · founder interpretation · hypothesis ·
aspiration · unknown · contradicted claim

The label governs what rewriting may do to the sentence. A hypothesis may be stated more
clearly, more honestly, and more precisely — it may never be restated as a fact. When a founder
asks for stronger wording on a hypothesis, that request is itself a finding: it marks the exact
place where they most want the evidence they do not have.
